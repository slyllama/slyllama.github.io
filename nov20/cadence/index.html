Sly Llama

Perfect Cadence

November 2020

(A portrait of Corinne, painted by the wonderful DPL.)

 

I participate in a rewarding Call of Cthulhu tabletop group where we solve Eldritch puzzles and battle horrifying monsters in the wonderful madness of ’30s America. I began with a more senior character; a kindly (if sometimes… interminably honest) pianist called Leander, but as I grew into the dynamic, I switched to playing his younger sister Corinne, a headstrong mechanic and businesswoman. She is, in many ways, a much more complex character with a past shrouded in mystery. This short piece of writing represents a casual attempt to delve into that history, and work out who the renowned engineer really is.

 

*

 

Comber, Northern Ireland, 1914. Excerpts from the journal of Caitlyn Langsford, civilian foreperson of the Eames Airstrip construction site.

13th Jan. Gravel wagons have finally arrived. We should have the surface material laid by mid-February, and then aircraft can begin to land. Admin has been pushing for the base to become active for mobilization ASAP, so work must pick up. Putting in a request for perhaps five more civilian workers to help progress the construction. (A few trees also need to be cleared to provide safe air passage.)

2nd Feb. Runway strip has been dug out much faster than expected; we’ll begin to shift the gravel in a week or so. Winston stopped by on tour, and has documented the facilities that we’ll need here to maintain his CA-12s. Speaking of CAs… heard back from W’s daughter for the first time after writing for replacement gasket seals; what a family, huh.

6th Feb. Hunter went for lunch and didn’t return for four-and-a-half hours. The young mason insisted that he’d been on his break for only ten minutes after finding a good meadow to relax in, just a quarter-league from the airstrip. No reprimands. Yet.

7th Feb. Visited the meadow for myself. Thought I could see glowing yellow strings tied between the weeds, but they disappeared as I approached the site. A beautiful, serene place, with bees buzzing from flower to flower in the early spring. Saw patches of stone peeking out of the grass, shining with glints of metal. Sat and watched for a little while and left – to find that night had already fallen. Need to investigate further, but I don’t like it.

9th Apr. I don’t like it. I don’t like it. I don’t like it. I don’t like it. I don’t like it.

 
 

Barth’s Inn, Scotland, 1921.

“Have you thought about it?” She tries to look Eve in the eye, but her gaze falls short.

“Only three months, Cori. You know I just need… a little more time. I need to work things out—”

“No, no, not— that. No! The artifact. What d’you say?” Nothing. Oh no… have I—

Eve laughs. “Of course! The artifact. You know I’ve always wanted to head west. And you’ve chased after worse things than a piece of metal. At least you’ll have someone to keep you out of trouble… Actually, my acceptance of this excursion hath verily become conditional.” As she speaks suddenly in the King’s English, her face becomes solemn; Corinne smiles shyly for the first time. “What is this thing? You make airplanes out of metal every day; why collect this piece for yourself? I knoweth not thy strange ways.” Putting down the stray washer that her fingers were twirling idly, Corinne finally looks up with a spark in her eyes. All business, Cori.

“Okay. Okay! So—” She shuffles through her satchel, pulling out a bundle of documents. The pages are neat, but their edges are furred with age and use. “So. Say you’re building a brick tower. We’d take, say, a trailer-load of bricks to the site and then trowel them all up in a batch, right? Now imagine a pulsing quartz crystal. It somehow manages to seq—”

“Wait, wait; you’re doing it again, Cori! Your bricks… the construction site.” Darn it; train of thought… all business. All business.

“Okay, the bricks. So, uh… so you’re getting these bricks out of the truck and propping them up. Then you head to the warehouse, pick up another load, and take care of that batch. Way faster than getting one person to retrieve one brick and trowel each at a time, right?

“But! What if that single person was extremely fast, collecting and placing bricks more quickly than a whole group of workers with a truck? Now you can use the truck and its workers for other jobs on the site. You could also have another fellow passing bricks up the tower, synchronized with that first bricklayer. Well, let’s say he’s collecting two bricks instead— you get it. The possibilities, Eve! It takes us two days to put a respirator together; with the right facilities and a synchronized speed like this, well…” As the engineer trailed off, she looks, slightly flustered, at her papers. Eve seemed… concerned.

“Cori… I know you’re excited. And yes, I’ll tag along. I— I’d like to follow you. But this artifact… you’re a woman of science, right? Of logic. I know a little more now, but your notes; the yellow string—”

“Do you see it, Eve? The time; the string… those were the bees – the bricklayers – going so fast from outside the reach of the metal’s aura that her eyes couldn’t track them. I don’t know how to explain it to you: I trust her. I wish she were still here now. But I have you, and I have these.” She motions to the documents, the metallic gleam still in her eyes.

Eve says nothing. Staring at the gesturing fingers, she places a gentle hand on Cori’s shoulder.

 
 

Boston, 1923.

Content warning: light horror; depictions of animal experimentation and transfiguration.

In the hot mist of the dark, dusky Boston cityscape, a dull glow brushes the sky, emanating from a foggy second-floor window just above the city’s Downtown district. What do the birds peeking through the glass see? A cluttered, dusty mess – books and papers strewn across the carpet, drinking-goblets and candles shuffled across the myriad chestnut surfaces, and a layer of dross over the metallic finishing.

“Y’know. Granddad said that it never looked like this in his time.”

“Oh?” Sparrow’s eye darts across.

Grey Crow mused. “Yes, yes. It was quite neat, apparently. Never many visitors, though. So that’s not changed, eh?” The two peer inward, and through the gloom they can faintly make out a figure bent over a table piled up with… something. Sparrow’s curiosity seems piqued.

“Say, Crow. You smell that?”

 

*

 

She wipes her brow with a flannel, and scrunches her nose as the stench rises up to meet her. Just part of the job, Corinne reminds herself. Just part of the job. But as she works, the casualties of her noble endeavour won’t stay out of her mind. Eve – things had been going so well. So warm. But she just couldn’t agree. Couldn’t agree? How‽ Before the two had stood the answer to so many questions: a small, engraved disk of metal gleaming in the ivy tangles. Eve had seen it first, in fact, as the two hiked along the northern trail, the glint in the distance catching the corner of her eye.

 

Unstrapping her satchel, Corinne had ventured alone into the alcove, prepared and determined, and insistent on keeping Eve at a safe distance. She kept her eyes on the floor, so as not to be taken in by the strange anomalies of time that she had become so convinced would occur. Pulling out an intricate-looking brass tank in her canvas bag, she gingerly lifted the disc – measuring no wider than her slender palm – into the container, and wound a pneumatic crank. With a hiss, a piston moved up into a smaller locking chamber, sealing the device, and a pump evacuated the air around the now hermetically sealed disc. Keeping her gaze on the tank, Corinne didn’t look up for a second as she retraced her steps. If she had, she would have seen streaks of grey settling into slower and slower pulses, until flying birds started to emerge from the arcs of colour. And had she looked carefully she might have even noticed the blurred edges of the trees sharpening as the effects of the wind became normalised. After a lifetime, she finally saw a figure out of the corner of her eye.

“Corinne. Corinne! Hey-o! it’s been about… an hour! Maybe two! Oh, that’s right… one, two, three, four, five—”

One. Two. Three. Four. Five. The seconds are just right. Now running up, the two embraced warmly. Corinne grabbed her hand, but her gaze was still somewhat stern.

“D’you think the vacuum device is working? Did you notice anything as I walked up? Any changes in pace or time?” Eve’s smile dropped a little. The engineer was still taken in by it all, even after seeing the metal in person.

“No, no, you were fine. Although… I’ve never seen you sprint as fast as you did just then in my life!” Corinne chuckled. Safe. At least they were safe. And Eve was here.

 

And now she isn’t here, Corinne recalls. She called me unhinged. And maybe she is unhinged, but the stakes for humanity are just too high. This device – the immutachron, she’d styled it – could change the world. It could change everything. Ten workers placing a thousand bricks in an hour is good, she thinks, but one worker placing a hundred bricks in a second is far, far better. She turns her attention fully to her work at the table.

The rats have done their job. Thanking them for their service, Corinne collects their shrunken corpses and dumps them into the overflowing burlap sack stuffed under the sink. Her soul is, truth be told, deeply disturbed by the sight of rapidly decaying flesh – but her mind, overtaken, cheers in delight. The immutachron is working, emaciating the creatures at a rapid rate. All as it should be. Roughly clearing the tabletop, she hauls a large, bulbous glass jar up onto the surface. Staring at her, floating gently in a briny liquid, is a black-bird, limbs protruding at awkward angles. A sad state indeed, but that was life – the device only operates on the organic. To extract time from elsewhere, I need a host with flesh and bone.

What the hell am I saying?

Never mind that. Not now. It’s past time to use the immutachron to do something real; tangible. She shuffles the wires and tubes strewn about the table, linking the jar’s contacts with the housing keeping the device from disrupting the time-flow of the neighbourhood. I’m going to save her, and then we can work on this together, forever. Not Eve. She called me unhinged. There was no place for those words at the Table of Time. No. I need Caitlyn.

She opens a valve on the locking device, allowing air to flow unilaterally across the disc. The counter connected in parallel begins to whir, a “zero” on its face – then a “one” and a “two”, faster and faster and faster as its mechanical dial ticks over rapidly. Ten people collecting a thousand pieces of data is good, she reflects, but one creature gathering a hundred packets in a second is far, far better. Humans were not infinite. If I can summarise someone in a paragraph, I must necessarily be able to encapsulate their whole being in that data. Now that is surely the engineering talk the endeavour requires.

“Three hundred.” “Nine hundred.” The data is coalescing. The moment of theosis has come.

Ripples emanate across the face of the liquid. The table is shaking, the grimy jars all clanking discordantly against one another. The numeric counter pops and jams as it reaches its limit, and the fluid in the jar begins to bubble. Corinne steps back a little, a steel spark in her eye.

First, a leg twitches, then a feather, and then a beak. The bird, slowly at first, and then in a more urgent pulsating motion, begins to writhe in cycles, its chest expanding and contracting to the rhythm of an unseen clock. As the gruesome gestures grow in vigour and violence, its loosening skin begins to wrinkle, feathers shrinking and flowing the waves of the bony undulations. The mass of flesh is shifting, travelling up the skeleton and leaving an angular outline in its wake.

As muscle merges with face, the bird’s visage distorts and transforms. The eye-sockets widen, beady eyeballs extending from pathetic cords out of the head. The beak softens and shortens, forming like putty around the mouth. Cartilage reshapes into a painfully… humanoid shape.

There is a whimper. A pitiful sound. The glint in her eye is gone. Corinne stumbles back further, transfixed by the sight of the bird-face-thing. Most of her being is paralysed in shock. This would make a wonderful radio play, quips her wit; and her heart reaches for the tender arms of an absent Eve, who – god damn it! – had been trying for months to get the engineer out of this spiral.

But there is no Eve: no warm smile, no cautioning look, no more sense of reason. Just…

Caitlyn.

It has to be Caitlin. The shell-like lips; the wisps of greasy hair; the misshapen arrangement of cartilage about the cheeks and chin… and it… she is whimpering. It is enough. It is too much. Corinne staggers forward again, breaking through a veil of insanity and reaching for the device. And as she grasps a bunch of cords and pipe-work to tear out the disgusting heart of this horrific experiment forever, she catches, in between the whimpers, a few, pitiful words rendered painfully by the black-bird.

“N— No. No, I— Please! Please. I don’t—

“I don’t like it here.”
