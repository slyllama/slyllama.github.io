Comber, Northern Ireland, 1914. Excerpts from the journal of Caitlyn Langsford, civilian foreperson of the Eames Airstrip construction site.

13th Jan. Gravel wagons have finally arrived. We should have the surface material laid by mid-February, and then aircraft can begin to land. Admin has been pushing for the base to become active for mobilization ASAP, so work must pick up. Putting in a request for perhaps five more civilian workers to help progress the construction. (A few trees also need to be cleared to provide safe air passage.)

2nd Feb. Runway strip has been dug out much faster than expected; we’ll begin to shift the gravel in a week or so. Winston stopped by on tour, and has documented the facilities that we’ll need here to maintain his CA-12s. Speaking of CAs… heard back from W’s daughter for the first time after writing for replacement gasket seals; what a family, huh.

6th Feb. Hunter went for lunch and didn’t return for four-and-a-half hours. The young mason insisted that he’d been on his break for only ten minutes after finding a good meadow to relax in, just a quarter-league from the airstrip. No reprimands. Yet.

7th Feb. Visited the meadow for myself. Thought I could see glowing yellow strings tied between the weeds, but they disappeared as I approached the site. A beautiful, serene place, with bees buzzing from flower to flower in the early spring. Saw patches of stone peeking out of the grass, shining with glints of metal. Sat and watched for a little while and left – to find that night had already fallen. Need to investigate further, but I don’t like it.

9th Apr. I don’t like it. I don’t like it. I don’t like it. I don’t like it. I don’t like it.

 
 

Barth’s Inn, Scotland, 1921.

“Have you thought about it?” She tries to look Eve in the eye, but her gaze falls short.

“Only three months, Cori. You know I just need… a little more time. I need to work things out—”

“No, no, not— that. No! The artifact. What d’you say?” Nothing. Oh no… have I—

Eve laughs. “Of course! The artifact. You know I’ve always wanted to head west. And you’ve chased after worse things than a piece of metal. At least you’ll have someone to keep you out of trouble… Actually, my acceptance of this excursion hath verily become conditional.” As she speaks suddenly in the King’s English, her face becomes solemn; Corinne smiles shyly for the first time. “What is this thing? You make airplanes out of metal every day; why collect this piece for yourself? I knoweth not thy strange ways.” Putting down the stray washer that her fingers were twirling idly, Corinne finally looks up with a spark in her eyes. All business, Cori.

“Okay. Okay! So—” She shuffles through her satchel, pulling out a bundle of documents. The pages are neat, but their edges are furred with age and use. “So. Say you’re building a brick tower. We’d take, say, a trailer-load of bricks to the site and then trowel them all up in a batch, right? Now imagine a pulsing quartz crystal. It somehow manages to seq—”

“Wait, wait; you’re doing it again, Cori! Your bricks… the construction site.” Darn it; train of thought… all business. All business.

“Okay, the bricks. So, uh… so you’re getting these bricks out of the truck and propping them up. Then you head to the warehouse, pick up another load, and take care of that batch. Way faster than getting one person to retrieve one brick and trowel each at a time, right?

“But! What if that single person was extremely fast, collecting and placing bricks more quickly than a whole group of workers with a truck? Now you can use the truck and its workers for other jobs on the site. You could also have another fellow passing bricks up the tower, synchronized with that first bricklayer. Well, let’s say he’s collecting two bricks instead— you get it. The possibilities, Eve! It takes us two days to put a respirator together; with the right facilities and a synchronized speed like this, well…” As the engineer trailed off, she looked, slightly flustered, at her papers. Eve looked… concerned.

“Cori… I know you’re excited. And yes, I’ll tag along. I— I’d like to follow you. But this artifact… you’re a woman of science, right? Of logic. I know a little more now, but your notes; the yellow string—”

“Do you see it, Eve? The time; the string… those were the bees – the bricklayers – going so fast from outside the reach of the metal’s aura that her eyes couldn’t track them. I don’t know how to explain it to you: I trust her. I wish she were still here now. But I have you, and I have these.” She motions to the documents, the metallic gleam still in her eyes.

Eve says nothing. Staring at the gesturing fingers, she places a gentle hand on Cori’s shoulder.

 

“I don’t like it.
I don’t like it.
I don’t like it.”
