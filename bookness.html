Sly Llama

The “Bookness” of the Thesis

(Download PDF.)

At the beginning of the seminar, we talked about the differences between the oral presentations we had given for architecture school in the past, and this new process of finishing the year with a single book. The nature of reading simultaneously offers an opportunity and demands a warning. Readers are much more likely to get ‘involved’ in a book than a presentation. The experience of reading a book is much more immediate and intimate—the words and images are projected directly in front of the reader, and absorbed in a private capacity. (Interestingly, only in recent times has this practice has become common. Saint Augustine (354 – 430) wrote of Saint Ambrose:

when he read, his eyes scanned the page and his heart sought out the meaning, but his voice was silent and his tounge was still. Anyone could approach him freely and guests were not commonly announced, so that often, when we came to visit him, we found him reading like this in silence, for he never read aloud.

Because of production costs prior to the printing-press, books carried an exorbitant value so the dissemination—also owing to literacy rates—was most often a shared experience.) The author has the opportunity to “spend a lot of time” with the reader: but that level of contact also makes it much easier to frustrate the reader by poor design and formatting decisions. Certain fonts, for instance—and especially many of the sans-serif ones—can be cumbersome to read, and large quantities risk putting off even the most determined reader from an otherwise well-crafted book.

Note: at the beginning of the seminar we were given a summary paper with a list of headings. I’ve used that document to sort my notes on the talk!

Binding

Cover material (soft-bound copy). While the hard-bound books (to be submitted to the library for storage) have plain, cloth-bound, and embossed covers, the paperback copies (printed for examination) allow for a lot more control over the design of the book-fronts so care should be taken when deciding on the paper type and thickness. However, apart from the cover, the interior ‘block’ should remain the same across all versions of the publication.

Cover image (soft-bound copy). Likewise, we get full control over the title image which features on the cover. It should probably not be too overbearing—there is a tendancy to exploit such an opportunity and design a cover which ‘reveals to much’ or, at the least, overwhelms the reader.

One volume or many? A couple of candidates whose work we observed during the talk chose to split their thesis contents across multiple small books. This suits some projects: but in doing so the author cedes a lot of control over the pacing, order, and sequence over to the reader, and control over the ebb and flow of the ‘paginated journey’ is diminished.

Additional artifacts (CDs, DVDs, notebooks, video, sound, QR codes, websites, etc.) These can be considered as candidates for inclusion, but again, they add additional pathways and complexities to both the physical form of the book (where is the CD stored) and the path of the reader's procedure (when do I look at the CD).

Contents page. Perhaps one of the most important point mentioned during the talk. The layout and composition as it is represented by the contents page is as important as the blocks of text themselves! The contents page reveals the structure and intent of the entire document, so it is important that it effectively and accurately represents the work. (There is more on this in the next section.)

Structure, contents, and sequencing

Chapters, parts, and sub-headings. When we give a spoken presentation to a group, we get to guide the audience through the project. Chapter headings are an apt replacement for this analogy of providing waypoints. They are pointers which serve to differentiate portions of the book, so for this reason, having the appropriate number and hierachy of sections is important.

Integration of text and images. Text and image should be treated as equally important, with both being capable of standing in their own right. The text doesn’t simply describe the images, and the pictures shouldn’t just highlight the words. We need to visually balance this hierachy on the page! For left-to-right books, the right side of the spread is the ‘prime page’, because it sits flat when the book is opened, looking directly at the reader. (In fact, this is why advertisements on the right side of magazines are often more expensive to purchase on the right side!)

Appendix. The appendix can house all of the material not contained in the main body of the thesis, allowing for a more succinct and cohesive core narrative. We have often seen corollaries being placed in footnotes, but they risk eventually turning the reader away from the footnotes, filled with tangents to the primary narrative as they are. Another potential option is to use marginalia to convey a sequence of tangential thoughts which accompany the text!

Rhythm of book: chapters, text, and blank pages. Much like an oratory presentation, the experience of reading a book constitutes a rhythm and flow of its own. Blank pages (as long as they are used sparingly) can be incorporated to break up sections and refresh the mind of the viewer.

Page stock: consistent or different? (This is one thing which especially applies to my own thesis, having portions of fully inked comics and heavily textual components.) If you work with a proper bindery, you have the opportunity to vary the kind of paper in your book (thickness, gloss, matte, etc). This can be good if, for example, you want to have inset colour plates in a more old fashioned manner.

Large pages and foldouts. It’s often the case that architects, artists, and designers want to have their prints reproduced as large as possible in the book! One solution is to have foldouts which double or triple the size of useable area. However, this technique has two caveats. Fold-outs are prone to damage, and they require a marginal amount of extra effort on the part of the reader which, if compounded across too many sheets, may drive the audience to ignore the fold-outs.

Different voices and writing styles: how are they identified? Some theses (between mine, for instance) have multiple ‘voices’, where dialogue, narrative, prose, or poetry is interspersed between more formal academic writing. We must consider how to visually balance these differing tones. (Some people in the exemplar works would manipulate the colour, font—serif vs sans-serif could be a good option---or add a border around the ‘voiced’ text frames.)

White space. An essential part of page layout, white space also helps to direct the rhythm and flow of the text—you don't want to include too much, either! One of its primary purposes is to restrict the width of a text block: there is a range of characters-per-line which is most comfortable for human reading.

Colour: why and how? With freedom comes responsibility! Just because there are a lot of opportunities to use colour doesn’t mean that they all should be taken—it should be used carefully and sparingly to promote structure and levels of information.

Hierachy of information. Again, not all text is equal. Some come under the main stream of the thesis narrative, whereas others accompany images or core text. This difference in structural hierachy needs to be differentiated in the very composition of the text.

Formatting

Font: sizes, serif, sans-serif, etc. Accessibility and acknowledgement of diversity are very important here. Different people have different sensibilities and requirements for reading text, so it is crucial that the contents of the page are easy to read and naturally flowing! Many people make the mistake of doing all of their compositing solely on a computer screen, resulting in improperly arranged and scaled spreads. Keep printing trial pages, and make sure to share them with plenty of friends in different situations, asking them for their feedback on the ergonomics of the type blocks as well as the contents of the text! One thing to note is that people are most comfortable reading the sorts of things they have seen in the past. A large block of sans-serif text on a printed page can feel unfamiliar and unwieldy simply because of the impetus of prior decisions to print in serifs.

Page numbers. They can tell more than just which page the reader is on; they can be another denomination of hierachy! Consider where they are placed, how large they are, and how they are formatted (Arabic numerals vs Roman numerals, for instance).

Grid layout. Pages don't have to be contained to a single column of text linearly interspersed with images; in the past, some people have subdivided their pages into 9 × 9 invisible grids, using them to orient images and text in a more fluid and creative manner. But there is a limit to this! Having an overly convoluted or complex layout can disturb the balance of the page.

Gutter space. More problems which can be avoided by printing out proof spreads. People have had a tendancy to make the internal gutters of the pages more narrow in the past. This can result in the text blocks falling into the center of the book, rendering them unreadable.

Full-bleed images: images which extend right to the edge of the page with no borders or margins! They can offer an impactful statement, and they also allow for the use of larger images, but should be used either sparingly or lavishly, else they risk disrupting the flow of the book.
